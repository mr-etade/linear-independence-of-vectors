Definitions

Linear Dependence

A set of vectors \(\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}\) is linearly dependent if:

\[c_1v_1 + c_2v_2 + \ldots + c_nv_n = 0\] has non-zero solutions

  • At least one coefficient is not zero
  • At least one vector can be written using the others

Example

Vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\) are dependent because \(2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix}\)

Linear Independence

A set of vectors \(\{v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n\}\) is linearly independent if:

\[c_1v_1 + c_2v_2 + \ldots + c_nv_n = 0\] only when all \(c_i = 0\)

  • No vector can be written using the others
  • Each vector adds new information

Example

Vectors \(\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}\) and \(\begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\) are independent

Testing Methods

Method 1: Solving the Equation

To test whether vectors are linearly independent, we set up and solve the equation:

\[ c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \ldots + c_n\mathbf{v}_n = \mathbf{0} \]

Step-by-Step Solution:

Step 1: Write the vector equation

For vectors \( \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} \), we have:

\[ c_1\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + c_2\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Expand into a system of equations

\[ \begin{cases} c_1(1) + c_2(2) = 0 \\ c_1(2) + c_2(4) = 0 \end{cases} \]

Which simplifies to:

\[ \begin{cases} c_1 + 2c_2 = 0 \quad \text{(Equation 1)} \\ 2c_1 + 4c_2 = 0 \quad \text{(Equation 2)} \end{cases} \]

Step 3: Solve the system

From Equation 1:

\[ c_1 = -2c_2 \]

Substitute into Equation 2:

\[ 2(-2c_2) + 4c_2 = 0 \]

\[ -4c_2 + 4c_2 = 0 \]

\[ 0 = 0 \]

Step 4: Analyze the solution

The system reduces to \( c_1 = -2c_2 \), meaning there are infinitely many non-zero solutions.

For example:

When \( c_2 = 1 \), then \( c_1 = -2 \)

This gives the non-trivial combination:

\[ -2\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \end{bmatrix} + 1\begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Conclusion: Since there exists a non-trivial solution where not all coefficients are zero, the vectors are linearly dependent.

Method 2: Matrix Method

To test linear independence using matrices:

  1. Form a matrix with the given vectors as columns.
  2. Row reduce the matrix to its row echelon form (REF).
  3. Analyze the pivot positions:
    • If each column has a pivot (leading 1), the vectors are linearly independent.
    • If at least one column does not have a pivot, then there are free variables and the vectors are linearly dependent.

Example

Given vectors: \[ \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 4 \\ 6 \end{bmatrix} \]

Create matrix \( A \) with these vectors as columns: \[ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 1: Row Reduce the Matrix

\[ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 2 & 4 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \\ 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Analyze

Only the first column has a leading 1 (pivot). The second column has no pivot.

\(\Rightarrow\) Free variable in the second column

\(\Rightarrow\) Vectors are linearly dependent

Method 3: Determinant Test (Square Matrices)

For \( n \) vectors in \( \mathbb{R}^n \), we test linear independence by computing the determinant of the matrix formed by the vectors as columns.

Key Rule:

  • If \( \det(A) = 0 \): Vectors are linearly dependent.
  • If \( \det(A) \neq 0 \): Vectors are linearly independent.

Example: Testing \( \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix} \)

Step 1: Construct the matrix \( A \) with the vectors as columns

\[ A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 \\ 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix} \]

Step 2: Compute the determinant of \( A \)

For a \( 2 \times 2 \) matrix \( \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix} \), the determinant is:

\[ \det(A) = ad - bc \]

Substituting the values:

\[ \det(A) = (3)(5) - (2)(1) = 15 - 2 = 13 \]

Step 3: Analyze the result

Since \( \det(A) = 13 \neq 0 \), the vectors are linearly independent.

Conclusion: The vectors \( \mathbf{v}_1 \) and \( \mathbf{v}_2 \) are independent because the determinant is non-zero.

Remarks

  • This method only works for square matrices (number of vectors = dimension of space).
  • For non-square matrices, use row reduction (Method 2) or the definition (Method 1).

Basis and Span

Span

All possible linear combinations of vectors

Basis

A linearly independent set that spans the space

Standard basis for \(\mathbb{R}^2\): \(\left\{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right\}\)

Dimension

The number of vectors in a basis for the space

  • \(\mathbb{R}^2\) has dimension 2
  • \(\mathbb{R}^3\) has dimension 3
